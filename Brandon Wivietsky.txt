When discussing online scraping and the legality of it, it's important to understand how the information is being accessed, is it really public or is the scraper illegally using the restricted data. The Computer Fraud and Abuse Act tried to address computer hacking, but its broad definition of "unauthorized access" was going against it. They utilized this term to justify the idea of scrapping under certain circumstances. In the end, scraping a website is illegal under the federal law, even when the data is visible to anyone with a browser. This issue was revealed in the hiQ Corporation. The LinkedIn case stated that the court ruled that scraping publicly accessible data did not violate the Privacy Act due to no evasion of any verification steps. But, this distinction is important to make due to simple internet activity and reporting would become illegal if gathering public information was an illegal act. Legality should be determined based on the availability of the data rather than it simply being gathered overtime automatically. Scraping data from sites that don't require a login and display information publicly should be dealt with separately from getting into a system that is protected due to the fact that it isn’t as secure as it should be with modern day technology.
