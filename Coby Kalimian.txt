Before scraping, consider both the public purpose or benefit of the project that you're monitoring, for instance., whether you benefit from having the data scraped in order to hold companies or governments accountable. An example would be our Airbnb project, in which we scraped the data to determine whether the company is in compliance with the law, as it would provide benefits to the public at large. On the other hand, if the same methods are used to scrape data to create a surveillance database, as was done by Clearview AI using people's images, there would likely be more significant harm than benefit to the person who never consented to having their data used in this way. Therefore, when considering scraping, you should determine if the intended goal will ultimately benefit the public and is not meant solely to use individuals' data for profit or control. Second, it is important to evaluate whether you are collecting personally identifiable data and how you will store or share those records. While a person may have a public profile through aspects of social media such as Facebook, scraping that person's public profile, in addition to scraping their public profile from other social media sites, could potentially expose sensitive information the person did not wish to reveal. The Cambridge Analytica incident is an example of how publicly scraped data from Facebook could be aggregated with data from other sources to influence or manipulate people in ways they were unaware of. A responsible guideline would be to minimize the collection of personal data to only what is necessary for the stated purpose, and to have a clear plan for how that data will be protected and eventually deleted.
