The way that short-term rentals (STRs) are regulated in San Francisco right now needs to change because it relies too much on someone other than the platforms (e.g., Airbnb) or the owners (i.e., hosts) to enforce regulations. As this project demonstrated, hosts do not use any automated mechanism to self-report their policy number. For example, a listing posted to the city's database with ID 16204265 had a policy number that was neither valid nor had the correct format. Only after the city receives a complaint do they begin their investigation. Thus, an owner can operate a listing without any regulatory scrutiny until someone finds out about the noncompliance and takes the time to file a complaint. This will result in a system that reacts instead of proactively provides oversight to protect the market for rental housing.


To improve the effectiveness of the enforcement of regulations to protect the rental housing market, Airbnb would be required to make sure that policy numbers match the official records of the city before the listing goes live. The requirement of validation by the platforms corresponds with the principle of acting in the public interest outlined in Principle 2 of the SWE Code of Ethics. Platforms providing STRs have an obligation to make sure that STRs comply with all city and county regulations rather than leaving it to external organizations to enforce compliance. Unless the platform has built-in accountability, the other means of enforcement will continue to rely upon unpaid efforts from members of the public and investigations into regulatory violations to ensure compliance with the laws designed to protect the housing market.
